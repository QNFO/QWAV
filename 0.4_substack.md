# The Assumption That's Costing Quantum Computing Billions — And the 48× Fix That Costs Nothing

**Rowan Brad Quni-Gudzinas · QWAV · May 2026**

---

Here's a question the quantum computing industry doesn't ask enough: *what if the qubit overhead isn't necessary?*

Let me back up.

Quantum computers are noisy. Physical qubits lose coherence, gates introduce errors, and without correction, your computation is random noise before it finishes. The standard solution — quantum error correction (QEC) — encodes each "logical" qubit across many physical qubits, using redundancy to detect and fix errors. Surface codes. Steane codes. Concatenated codes.

The problem is the overhead. A single useful logical qubit can require 100 physical qubits. Some estimates go to 10,000. Every one of those extra qubits needs control, readout, and cooling. The dilution refrigerators required to reach millikelvin temperatures provide about 50 microwatts of cooling. Commercial 4 K cryocoolers provide about 1 watt. That's a 20,000× gap — and it's structural. You can't fix it with better refrigerators.

The industry has spent billions on this architecture. And it's built on an assumption so fundamental that almost nobody questions it: **that space is continuous.**

---

## The Geometry Question

Here's the thing. Standard quantum circuits are built on continuous manifolds — Archimedean geometry, where distances add normally: the distance from A to C is at most the distance from A to B plus the distance from B to C. This is the triangle inequality you learned in school.

But there's another kind of geometry — **ultrametric geometry** — where distances work differently. In an ultrametric space, distances obey the *strong* triangle inequality: the distance from A to C is at most the *maximum* of the distances from A to B and B to C. Everything is organized in trees, not grids.

What happens when you build quantum circuits on trees instead of grids?

Errors become **geometrically confined**. In a continuous space, an error at point A can propagate — it adds up with errors at B and C, accumulating across the circuit. In a tree, errors at different branches are isolated by the tree structure. They don't accumulate. They attenuate naturally.

This is **passive fault tolerance** — error suppression that's built into the geometry of the computation itself. No extra qubits. No active correction. The math handles it.

---

## The Bruhat-Tits Tree

The specific tree structure we use comes from p-adic number theory — the Bruhat-Tits tree. It's a well-studied mathematical object that's been hiding in pure math for decades, waiting for someone to ask: "what if we compute on this?"

Here's how it works. Logical qubits are encoded at the leaves of the tree — the bottom layer where computation happens. Physical quantum states live higher up in the tree structure. Between them sits a holographic tensor network of **perfect tensors** — mathematical objects that enforce the isometric relationship between the logical and physical layers. The tree geometry does the rest.

And critically: this works at **4 K**, not millikelvin. The same temperature as a standard cryocooler. No dilution refrigerator required.

## The q-Ary Scatter

But there's a second piece — and this is the one I want to focus on, because it's the most accessible "wow" in our results.

Every atom has more than two energy levels. In standard quantum computing, you pick two levels to be your |0⟩ and |1⟩, and you treat the rest as nuisance states to be avoided. Fair enough — keeping things binary simplifies your control.

But those extra levels are already there. They're not costing you anything. What if you *used* them?

That's the **q-ary scatter**. Instead of encoding one logical bit across two leaf states (q = 2), we spread it across more — q = 4, q = 16, q = 64, all the way up to q = 128. The tree's ultrametric property amplifies the protection as the logical information spreads across more structural positions.

The computational result, from Sprint 4 of our ultrametric_v2 validation:

> **At q = 128, the logical error rate drops by approximately 48× compared to q = 2 — at the same tree depth, the same physical error rate, and with zero additional physical qubits.**

Forty-eight times better. Zero extra cost.

## Why This Is Different

Every other approach to error reduction in quantum computing follows the same pattern: *add more hardware.* Surface codes need more qubits. Concatenation needs more gates. Active feedback needs more control electronics. All of it generates more heat. All of it costs more money. All of it makes the engineering harder.

The q-ary scatter is the opposite. It asks: *what do you already have that you're not using?* The answer, it turns out, is quite a lot. Every atom in your quantum processor has hyperfine levels that you've been ignoring. Use them, and you get a 48× improvement for free.

This isn't a tradeoff. It's a pure win.

## The Full Architecture

The q-ary scatter isn't isolated — it's part of a coherent architectural thesis that our computational validation has now tested across seven sprints:

1. **Ternary (p = 3) trees** — symmetric passive protection for both logical states. Binary (p = 2) protects only one state; ternary fixes that asymmetry while keeping the tree compact.
2. **q-ary scatter** — 48× additional error reduction using existing hyperfine levels, at zero qubit cost.
3. **Concatenation with active QEC is redundant** — we tested it. Surface codes and Steane codes added on top of the tree provide zero additional benefit. The geometry already does the work.
4. **40-atom neutral atom hardware** — at depth d = 3, you need 40 atoms arranged in a ternary tree. This is within demonstrated experimental capabilities (Harvard has published 256-atom programmable arrays). Rydberg blockade gates handle the tree-vertex operations. Operation at 4 K.

## The Honest Part

I want to be clear about what this is and isn't.

This is **computational validation**, not experimental. We've simulated these circuits — we haven't built them. The results are strong, reproducible, and published with full code. But they're simulations of a physical architecture, not measurements from a physical device.

The conventional approach — surface codes on superconducting qubits — has billions of dollars and decades of experimental refinement behind it. We have seven sprints of Python simulations and a mathematical framework that's been hiding in number theory since the 1960s.

I'm not asking you to believe the tree geometry is better than surface codes. I'm asking you to look at the numbers and ask: *what if the bottleneck isn't qubits — it's geometry?* Because if it is, the solution costs nothing.
