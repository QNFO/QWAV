## Executive Summary

**Direct answer: No one else is building quantum computers on ultrametric (Bruhat-Tits tree) geometry.**

There are zero known competitors pursuing the same thesis — replacing Archimedean (continuous) geometry with ultrametric (tree-based) geometry for quantum computing hardware, software, or AI. The space is empty.

However, there are adjacent research communities working on components that either converge toward QWAV's thesis from different starting points, or provide the mathematical and experimental foundations QWAV builds upon. This document maps those adjacencies, differentiates QWAV's position, and identifies the structural reasons the space remains empty — which is itself a strategic insight.

**If this null result holds under external scrutiny, QWAV's thesis is genuinely novel.** The question becomes not "who else is doing this" but "why isn't anyone else doing this, and what does that say about the opportunity?"

**Key insight:** Holographic quantum codes are the closest existing work to QWAV's encoding. Both use perfect tensors. Both organize qubits in tree-like structures. The critical difference: holographic codes use hyperbolic geometry (continuous, Riemannian), while QWAV uses ultrametric geometry (discrete, p-adic). This difference is not cosmetic — it changes the error propagation behavior (strong triangle inequality vs. regular triangle inequality) and the operational temperature (holographic codes still require millikelvin; QWAV operates at 4 K).

**Differentiation:** "Holographic codes demonstrated that geometry matters for error correction. QWAV asks: what if you use the *right* geometry?"

## 2. Why Nobody Else Is Doing This

The competitive landscape is empty for structural reasons, not because the idea is bad:

### 2.1 The Silo Problem

The p-adic mathematical physics community and the quantum computing community do not talk to each other.

- **P-adic community:** Publishes in journals like *p-Adic Numbers, Ultrametric Analysis and Applications*. Reads by mathematicians and mathematical physicists. Does not attend quantum computing conferences.
- **Quantum computing community:** Publishes in *Physical Review X*, *Quantum*, *Nature Physics*. Reads by physicists and engineers. Does not read p-adic journals.
- **The bridge doesn't exist yet.** Zúñiga-Galindo's upcoming Mexico City conference is one of the first attempts to build it.

This silo is QWAV's structural moat. The mathematics needed to propose ultrametric quantum computing — Bruhat-Tits trees, p-adic analysis, strong triangle inequality — lives in a separate intellectual silo from the people who build quantum computers. As long as those communities don't talk, QWAV's thesis remains invisible to potential competitors.

### 2.2 The Assumption Problem

The assumption that "space is continuous" is so deeply embedded in physics that most physicists don't recognize it as an assumption. It's part of the default cognitive architecture of the field.

- Quantum mechanics was built on continuous Hilbert spaces
- Quantum field theory assumes continuous spacetime
- Quantum circuits are designed on continuous manifolds
- Nobody asks "what if space isn't continuous?" because the question doesn't occur to them

This is precisely the pattern of Kuhnian paradigm shifts: the old paradigm doesn't get disproven — it just stops being the only way to think about the problem. QWAV's thesis is a paradigm proposal, not a technical improvement within the existing paradigm.

### 2.3 The Credential Barrier

Proposing a new geometry for quantum computing requires:
- Deep knowledge of p-adic mathematics (rare in quantum computing)
- Deep knowledge of quantum error correction (rare in p-adic math)
- Willingness to work outside institutional frameworks (QWAV's structural position)

The intersection of these three is vanishingly small. Most people with the mathematical background don't have the quantum computing background, and vice versa. And those who have both are generally inside institutions that reward incremental progress within established paradigms, not paradigm-level proposals.

### 2.4 What Would Change This

The competitive landscape would change if:

1. **Zúñiga-Galindo's conference succeeds** in bringing p-adic mathematicians and physicists into the same room
2. **A major quantum computing lab** publishes a paper on tree-based error correction
3. **A neutral atom group** demonstrates a Bruhat-Tits tree circuit (even at depth $d=2$)
4. **A prominent theorist** (Preskill, Kitaev, Aaronson) publicly engages with the ultrametric thesis

QWAV's strategy — publish first, publish openly, let substance speak — is designed to establish prior art and thought leadership before any of these triggers fire. The null competitive landscape is a window. The window won't stay open forever.

## 4. Honest Assessment: Strengths and Vulnerabilities

### Strengths
- Genuinely novel thesis — the space is empty
- Computational validation demonstrates the effect exists in simulation
- Public disclosure establishes prior art and prevents patent ambush
- Aligned with existing hardware (neutral atom platforms already exist)
- The "moat" (silo between p-adic math and QC communities) is structural, not accidental

### Vulnerabilities
- No experimental validation (simulation only)
- No institutional affiliation (credential barrier with some audiences)
- No team (single point of failure)
- P-adic community is small and under-resourced (few collaborators available)
- If a major lab validates the thesis, they have resources QWAV doesn't (this is the "first-mover disadvantage" — you prove the path, someone else walks it faster)

### Mitigation Strategy
- Publish everything openly with DOIs → establish priority
- Disclose patent claims publicly → create prior art blocking others
- Target neutral atom labs for collaboration → if someone else builds it, QWAV still gets credit for the architecture
- Build the narrative library (M1-M16) → thought leadership is defensible even without hardware
