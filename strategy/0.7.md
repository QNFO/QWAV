## Executive Summary

MIT researchers (Nature Photonics, May 2026) demonstrated a technique for creating 3D nanostructures by writing a pattern in a hydrogel scaffold using visible-light photopatterning, then shrinking it by 2,000× through isotropic dehydration — taking features from ~800 nm down to sub-100 nm. This is relevant to QWAV's patent Claim 4 (Bruhat-Tits tree hierarchical lattice) because it provides a potential fabrication method for the physical tree structure. However, the technique produces embedded photonic structures in hydrogel, not free-standing atomic arrays. The relevance is indirect but real — it's a fabrication pathway for a *photonic* implementation of the tree lattice, distinct from the neutral atom pathway.

**Verdict:** Worth monitoring. Not actionable for the primary neutral atom pathway (which uses reconfigurable optical tweezers, not fabricated structures). But if a photonic quantum computing pathway is pursued (visible-light photons, tree-structured waveguides), this technique provides a fabrication method. Add to patent Claim 4 as an alternative embodiment.

## 1. The MIT Implosion Carving Technique

### What They Did

**Technique:** "Implosion carving" — 3D nanofabrication via:

1. **Write:** Pattern a hydrogel scaffold using visible-light photopatterning. Features are written at ~800 nm resolution — achievable with standard optical lithography.
2. **Shrink:** Dehydrate the hydrogel. The scaffold shrinks isotropically (uniformly in all directions) by approximately 2,000×.
3. **Result:** The original ~800 nm features shrink to sub-100 nm — the scale of visible-light photonic devices.

**Key advantage:** The isotropic shrinkage preserves the *shape* of the pattern. If you write a tree structure at 800 nm, you get a tree structure at sub-100 nm. The geometry is preserved; only the scale changes.

### What This Means

| Conventional Nanofabrication | Implosion Carving |
|:----------------------------|:------------------|
| Requires expensive lithography (EUV, electron beam) | Uses visible-light photopatterning (cheap) |
| Sub-100 nm features require specialized equipment | Sub-100 nm achieved via shrinkage, not direct writing |
| 3D structures are difficult (layer-by-layer) | 3D structures written in volume, then shrunk |
| Slow, expensive, clean-room required | Potentially faster and cheaper |

## 2. Relevance to QWAV

### 2.1 Patent Claim 4 (from `strategy/0.2.md`)

Claim 4 covers: *"The quantum computing device of claim 1, wherein the Bruhat-Tits tree is implemented as a hierarchical lattice structure fabricated via..."*

The MIT technique provides a specific fabrication method to include in this claim:

*"...wherein the hierarchical lattice structure is fabricated by photopatterning a hydrogel scaffold in the shape of the tree, followed by isotropic dehydration to reduce the feature size to sub-100 nanometer dimensions."*

### 2.2 Photonic Quantum Computing Pathway

QWAV's primary hardware pathway is neutral atom arrays (40 atoms, $d=3$ tree, Rydberg blockade gates). This uses reconfigurable optical tweezers — nothing is "fabricated" in the traditional sense. The atoms are trapped in place by light.

But a *photonic* implementation — using photons rather than atoms as qubits, confined in tree-structured waveguides — would require physical fabrication. The MIT technique is a candidate fabrication method for photonic tree circuits.

### 2.3 Mapping: Tree Features → Photopatterning → Shrinkage

| Tree Element | Conventional Implementation | MIT Implosion Implementation |
|:-------------|:---------------------------|:------------------------------|
| Tree nodes (vertices) | Optical trap sites | Waveguide junctions written at 800 nm → shrink to ~40 nm |
| Tree edges (connections) | Free-space optical paths | Waveguides written at 800 nm → shrink to ~40 nm width |
| Leaf encoding sites | Atom positions | Photon detection sites at tree boundary |
| Perfect tensor operations | Rydberg blockade gates | Directional couplers at waveguide junctions |
| 4 K operation | Cryocooler | Cryocooler (photonic circuits also benefit from low temperature) |

## 3. Feasibility Assessment

### What Would Need to Be True

For the MIT technique to serve as a QWAV fabrication pathway:

1. **Tree-compatible hydrogel chemistry.** The hydrogel must support the specific 3D pattern of a Bruhat-Tits tree — a hierarchically branching structure, not a periodic lattice. The MIT paper demonstrated arbitrary 3D shapes, suggesting this is feasible.

2. **Optical-quality material post-shrinkage.** The shrunken hydrogel must have optical properties suitable for waveguiding (refractive index contrast, low scattering loss). Hydrogels are not typical optical materials; doping with high-index nanoparticles may be required.

3. **Sub-100 nm waveguide dimensions.** The shrunk features must be on the order of visible-light wavelengths (~40-80 nm for blue/green light) to support single-mode waveguiding. The 2,000× shrinkage from 800 nm → 40 nm hits this target.

4. **Cryogenic compatibility.** The hydrogel must not crack, delaminate, or lose optical properties at 4 K. This is a significant unknown — hydrogels are water-based, and freezing typically destroys them. A cryogenic-compatible gel chemistry would be a separate research problem.

5. **Integration with photon sources/detectors.** The tree structure must be coupled to single-photon sources and detectors at the leaf nodes. This adds fabrication complexity beyond the tree itself.

### Realism Score

| Factor | Feasibility |
|:-------|:------------|
| 3D tree patterning at ~800 nm | ✅ Demonstrated (MIT) |
| Isotropic shrinkage to sub-100 nm | ✅ Demonstrated (MIT) |
| Optical-quality material | ⚠️ Unknown — hydrogels not typical waveguide materials |
| Cryogenic compatibility (4 K) | ⚠️ Unknown — hydrogel freezing likely destructive |
| Single-mode waveguiding at visible λ | ⚠️ Plausible at 40 nm dimensions but unverified |
| Photon source/detector integration | ❌ Not addressed |
| Overall near-term feasibility | **Low — but worth citing as a potential pathway** |

## 4. Strategic Positioning

### 4.1 Strengthen Patent Claim 4

The MIT technique should be cited in patent Claim 4 as a *specific* fabrication method. The claim should read:

*"...wherein the hierarchical lattice structure is fabricated via one of: (a) photopatterning of a scaffold material followed by isotropic size reduction; (b) direct-write lithography; (c) self-assembly of nanostructured materials; or (d) any combination thereof."*

This broadens the claim beyond any single fabrication technique.

### 4.2 Diversify Hardware Pathways

QWAV currently has one hardware pathway: neutral atom arrays. If neutral atom labs don't engage (P32), or if Rydberg blockade gates prove problematic, having a second pathway — photonic quantum computing on fabricated tree structures — provides a backup.

### 4.3 Visibility Signal

Citing a 2026 Nature Photonics paper in QWAV's patent or next paper revision signals awareness of cutting-edge fabrication. It shows that the tree architecture is not purely mathematical — there are emerging fabrication techniques that could realize it physically.

## 6. Broader Context: Why Fabrication Matters Now

The standard objection to QWAV's thesis is: "this is mathematics, not engineering — where's the hardware?" The 40-atom neutral atom design (D10) is one answer. The MIT implosion carving technique is a second, complementary answer: even if neutral atoms prove difficult, there's an emerging fabrication pathway for the physical tree structure.

The strategic principle: **always have at least one concrete physical instantiation pathway.** The neutral atom pathway is primary (40 atoms, existing labs, 4 K). The photonic pathway is secondary (future fabrication, lower TRL, but geometrically elegant). Together, they prevent the "it's just math" objection from sticking.
