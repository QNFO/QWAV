## Executive Summary

QWAV patent claims are already public on GitHub (`QNFO/QWAV`, `strategy/0.2.md`, 25 claims across 4 domains) and computational validation papers are published on Zenodo (DOIs: 10.5281/zenodo.20134944, 10.5281/zenodo.20208437). This document analyzes:

1. Whether this public disclosure is strategically harmful or actually beneficial (defensive publishing)
2. Whether the US 12-month grace period provides a backdoor for international filings (it does not)
3. What actions, if any, should be taken — mapped against QWAV's core constraints (solo founder, $0 raised, open-access strategy, no peer review, substance-first evaluation)

**Bottom line:** The public disclosure is *strategically coherent* with QWAV's open-access, substance-first thesis. It functions as zero-cost defensive publishing that prevents others from patenting the core innovations. The main sacrifice — loss of patent rights in absolute-novelty jurisdictions (Europe, China) — has negligible practical impact for a solo founder with $0 raised and a 0% historical patent conversion rate. A single US provisional filing (~$75-$325 micro-entity) within the 12-month window preserves optionality at near-zero cost and is recommended as a hedge. International patent protection was never realistic under current constraints, and the public disclosure does not meaningfully change that calculus.

## 2. Defensive Publishing: Is Public Disclosure a Bug or a Feature?

### 2.1 What Defensive Publishing Achieves

Defensive publishing is the practice of publicly disclosing an invention to create **prior art** — preventing others from obtaining a patent on the same invention. It is a deliberate strategy used by major technology companies (IBM, Google, Tesla) and open-source projects.

**How it works for QWAV:**

1. The public GitHub repo (`QNFO/QWAV`) with timestamped commits serves as evidence of invention date and public availability
2. The detailed claims in `strategy/0.2.md` describe the invention with sufficient specificity to establish prior art
3. The companion code repo (`QNFO/ultrametric-error-confinement`) provides enabling disclosure — someone skilled in the art could reproduce the invention
4. If any third party attempts to patent the Bruhat-Tits tree encoding, ultrametric error confinement, or Q-PNA methods, the GitHub commit history and Zenodo DOIs serve as prior art to invalidate their claims

**Result:** No one else can fence QWAV out of its own invention space.

### 2.2 Alignment with QWAV Strategy

The QWAV thesis is fundamentally **open-access**. Core constraints (from `PROJECT STATE.md`, Section 3):

| Constraint | How Defensive Publishing Aligns |
|:-----------|:-------------------------------|
| **No peer review** | Public disclosure + reader evaluation is the QWAV publication model. Defensive publishing is the same model applied to IP: publish first, let substance speak. |
| **Substance-first evaluation** | Defensive publishing says: "Here is the invention. Judge it on its merits. If it's valuable, the disclosure itself protects it." This is philosophically identical to the QWAV publishing strategy. |
| **Written-only path** | Defensive publishing is entirely written — no patent prosecution, no attorney negotiations, no examiner interviews. |
| **Solo founder, $0 raised** | Defensive publishing costs $0. Patent prosecution costs $10K-$100K+. The resource constraint makes defensive publishing the *only* realistic option for international protection. |
| **Open-access publishing** | Defensive publishing IS open-access IP. Consistent with Zenodo, ResearchGate, SSRN, arXiv strategy. |

**Strategic coherence:** The QWAV strategy is to publish everything openly and let substance create value. Defensive publishing is that same strategy applied to intellectual property. Filing patents — especially international ones — would be the *departure* from strategy, not the alignment.

### 2.3 What Is Sacrificed

| Sacrificed | Practical Impact for QWAV |
|:-----------|:--------------------------|
| Ability to exclude others from practicing the invention | QWAV has no manufacturing capability, no lab, no team. The ability to exclude others is a theoretical right with no practical enforcement mechanism for a solo founder with $0. |
| Licensing revenue from exclusive patent rights | The EV analysis in `0.2.md` showed negative expected value in all scenarios. Licensing revenue requires (a) a granted patent, (b) an infringer with revenue, (c) litigation budget. None of these exist. |
| "IP assets" for investment attractiveness | Patents are signaling devices for VCs. But QWAV's strategy explicitly rejects the VC path (no live pitching, no networking, written-only). Patent signaling value is zero for the chosen path. |
| International patent rights in absolute-novelty countries | See Section 3. These were never realistic under current constraints. |

### 2.4 The Counterargument (And Why It's Weak for QWAV)

**Counterargument:** "If QWAV succeeds and attracts collaborators/funding, having filed patents would be valuable for negotiations, licensing, and defensive positioning against competitors."

**Why it's weak for QWAV specifically:**
1. The historical conversion rate is 0% — ~18 provisionals filed, zero converted. The pattern suggests filing without conversion is a recurring cost sink, not a strategic asset.
2. The QWAV strategy explicitly rejects the pathways where patents matter most (VC funding, institutional partnerships, exclusive licensing).
3. If QWAV *does* attract collaborators/funding, a US provisional can still be filed within the 12-month window (see Section 4).
4. The substance of the work — computational validation, mathematical foundations, published papers — is the real asset. Patents are legal fences around land you can't farm.

## 3. The US 12-Month Grace Period: No International Backdoor

### 3.1 How the US Grace Period Works

Under 35 U.S.C. $\S$ 102(b)(1) (AIA), a disclosure made by the inventor or someone who obtained the subject matter from the inventor is **not prior art** if it was made **one year or less before** the effective filing date of the claimed invention.

This means: if you publicly disclose on Day 0, and file a US patent application on Day 300, the Day 0 disclosure does NOT count as prior art against your own application. You get a 12-month window.

### 3.2 The "Backdoor" Question

**Question:** If I publicly disclose now, wait 11 months, file a US provisional, then file a PCT (Patent Cooperation Treaty) application claiming priority to that US provisional — does the 12-month US grace period "travel" with the PCT to protect me internationally?

**Answer: No. It does not.**

The sequence:
```
GitHub/Zenodo disclosure (2026-05-12, Day 0)
        ↓
File US provisional (2027-04-01, Day ~324 — within 12-month US window)
        ↓
File PCT claiming priority to US provisional (priority date: 2027-04-01, Day ~324)
        ↓
Enter national/regional phases (Europe, China, Japan, etc.)
```

**What happens in the European phase:** The EPO examiner searches for prior art before the priority date (2027-04-01). They find the GitHub repo and Zenodo papers from 2026-05-12 — 11 months BEFORE the priority date. Under Article 54 of the European Patent Convention (absolute novelty), any public disclosure before the filing/priority date destroys novelty. Your OWN disclosure is prior art against your OWN application.

**The US grace period is purely domestic.** It does not "attach" to the application and travel through the PCT. Each national/regional phase applies its own patentability standards independently.

### 3.3 Country-by-Country Breakdown

#### Countries WITH Grace Periods (Public Disclosure Before Filing Is Forgiven, Within Limits)

| Country/Jurisdiction | Grace Period | Key Conditions |
|:---------------------|:-------------|:---------------|
| **United States** | 12 months | AIA $\S$ 102(b)(1) — inventor's own disclosure, or disclosure by someone who obtained from inventor |
| **Canada** | 12 months | Applicant's own disclosure |
| **Australia** | 12 months | Applicant's own disclosure; also recognizes public use |
| **Korea (South)** | 12 months | Applicant's own disclosure (expanded from 6 months in 2015) |
| **Brazil** | 12 months | Applicant's own disclosure (new IP law) |
| **Mexico** | 12 months | Applicant's own disclosure |
| **Argentina** | 1 year | Applicant's own disclosure |
| **Japan** | 6 months | Very limited — applies only to: (1) testing publication, (2) presentation at designated academic societies, (3) disclosure against inventor's will, (4) exhibition at government-organized exhibitions. Does NOT cover voluntary GitHub/Zenodo publication. |
| **Russia** | 6 months | Applicant's own disclosure |
| **Philippines** | 12 months | Applicant's own disclosure |
| **Singapore** | 12 months | Applicant's own disclosure |

**Implication for QWAV:** If a US provisional is filed within 12 months (by ~2027-05-12), patent protection can still be obtained in: US, Canada, Australia, Korea, Brazil, Mexico, and a few smaller markets — covering most of the Americas and parts of Asia-Pacific.

#### Countries With NO Grace Period (Absolute Novelty)

| Country/Jurisdiction | Rule | Practical Impact |
|:---------------------|:-----|:-----------------|
| **European Patent Office (EPC — 39 member states)** | Article 54: Absolute novelty. ANY public disclosure anywhere in the world, in any language, by anyone (including the inventor), before the filing date, destroys patentability. | European patent rights are **already lost** for the inventions disclosed on GitHub and Zenodo. |
| **China** | Absolute novelty. No grace period for inventor's own disclosure (narrow exception for disclosures at government-recognized international exhibitions). | Chinese patent rights are **already lost**. |
| **India** | Absolute novelty. Very narrow exceptions (government exhibitions, learned society publications with prior notice to the Controller). | Indian patent rights are **already lost**. |
| **Most of Southeast Asia** (Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam) | Absolute novelty. | Already lost. |
| **Most of Africa** | Absolute novelty (varies by country). | Already lost. |
| **Middle East** (varies) | Mixed — some with limited grace periods, most with absolute novelty. | Mostly lost. |

### 3.4 Why This Sacrifice Is Acceptable for QWAV

The markets with absolute novelty (Europe, China, India) represent the *most expensive* jurisdictions for patent prosecution:

| Jurisdiction | Estimated Cost (Filing + Prosecution + Maintenance, 20-year life) |
|:-------------|:------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **European Patent (EPC)** | $35,000-$60,000+ (validation in 3-5 member states; each additional state adds $2,000-$5,000) |
| **China** | $15,000-$30,000 |
| **India** | $5,000-$10,000 |
| **Japan** | $20,000-$40,000 |

**Total for minimal international coverage:** $75,000-$140,000+

For a solo founder with $0 raised and negative expected value from patenting (per `0.2.md` EV analysis), these costs are prohibitive regardless of disclosure timing. The public disclosure did not *cause* the loss of international rights — the resource constraint made them inaccessible from the start. The disclosure merely made explicit what was already implicit: international patent prosecution was never on the table.

## 4. Strategic Recommendation

### 4.1 Two Coherent Paths

#### Path A — Pure Defensive Publishing (Do Nothing More)

**Action:** Accept that the public GitHub repo + Zenodo papers already serve as defensive publication. Do not file any patent applications. Rely on prior art to prevent others from patenting the core innovations.

**Pros:**
- Cost: $0
- Philosophically aligned with QWAV open-access strategy
- No patent prosecution burden (no attorney fees, no office actions, no maintenance fees)
- No distraction from the core work (computational validation, publishing, outreach)
- Consistent with historical pattern (0% conversion rate)

**Cons:**
- No offensive patent rights anywhere — cannot exclude others from practicing the invention
- If QWAV later attracts funding/collaborators and wants patent protection, the US window closes 2027-05-12
- Some funders/investors expect patent filings as a signal (though QWAV's strategy explicitly rejects this audience)

#### Path B — File ONE US Provisional (Hedge)

**Action:** File a single US provisional patent application within the 12-month window (before ~2027-05-12). Use the specification from `strategy/0.2.md` as the basis. Cost: $75 (micro-entity fee, if qualifying) or $325 (small entity fee).

**Pros:**
- Cost: $75-$325 — near-zero compared to any alternative
- Preserves the OPTION to convert to a US non-provisional and/or PCT for 12 more months (until ~2028-04)
- Secures a US priority date, enabling filings in grace-period countries (Canada, Australia, Korea, Brazil)
- Does not require conversion — can still let it expire if circumstances haven't changed
- A provisional does not require formal claims — the specification in `0.2.md` is already more detailed than many filed provisionals
- Provides a "patent pending" designation that has some signaling value even under the written-only strategy

**Cons:**
- $75-$325 cost (trivial)
- Time to prepare and file (~1-2 days of focused work)
- Psychological: having a provisional "on the clock" creates a 12-month decision pressure to convert or abandon (this can be reframed as healthy optionality, not pressure)

### 4.2 Recommendation: Path B

**File one US provisional before the 12-month window closes.** Rationale:

1. **The cost is negligible** ($75-$325) compared to the optionality it buys
2. **It does not require abandoning the defensive publishing strategy** — the GitHub repo and Zenodo papers continue to serve as prior art
3. **It does not commit to the expensive non-provisional/PCT path** — that decision is deferred 12 months
4. **It preserves US rights and grace-period-country rights** — which are the only markets where patent protection was ever realistic
5. **It aligns with the "file ONE or ZERO" recommendation** from the `0.2.md` EV analysis
6. **It converts the clock from a deadline into an option** — the 12-month window becomes a period of optionality rather than a countdown to lost rights

### 4.3 What the Provisional Should Cover

The `strategy/0.2.md` document already contains:
- 25 claims across 4 domains (apparatus, method, system)
- Supporting experimental data (computational validation from Tier 0 and Tier 1 papers)
- Technical specification with mathematical foundations
- Cross-references to companion code repository

**What a provisional needs (minimal):**
- A written description of the invention sufficient to enable someone skilled in the art to make and use it
- No formal claims required (though including them is best practice)
- No formal drawings required (though including them strengthens the filing)
- No oath or declaration required

**What `0.2.md` already provides:**
- Detailed claims (25 claims, claims 1-25, with dependencies)
- Description of the invention (Sections 2.1-2.4 of `0.2.md`)
- Supporting data (references to Tier 0 and Tier 1 papers with DOIs)
- The companion code repo provides enabling disclosure for the software methods

**The gap:** A provisional filing requires a specific format (specification with background, summary, detailed description, claims/abstract). `0.2.md` is a strategy document, not a patent specification. A conversion step is needed — but it's a formatting exercise, not a content-creation exercise. All the substance already exists.
