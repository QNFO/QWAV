## Executive Summary

Most quantum computing papers benchmark against other quantum computing architectures. They rarely benchmark against classical baselines, and when they do, the baseline is often a straw man — a naive classical algorithm that nobody would actually use.

Ultrametric_v2 did the opposite. Sprint 5 benchmarked the ternary ($p = 3$) tree architecture against a **classical tree baseline** under two noise regimes — uncorrelated (i.i.d.) and correlated. The tree lost at i.i.d. noise. It won under correlated noise. Both results were reported honestly.

This is a credibility differentiator. The honest null result (tree loses at i.i.d.) makes the positive result (tree wins under correlation) more trustworthy. It signals that QWAV's validation is scientific — it tells you when the architecture *doesn't* work, not just when it does.

## 1. The Benchmarking Problem in Quantum Computing

### 1.1 How Most QC Papers Benchmark

The standard approach in quantum computing validation:

1. Pick a quantum architecture (e.g., surface codes on superconducting qubits)
2. Simulate it under idealized conditions
3. Compare it against other quantum architectures (e.g., cat codes, color codes)
4. Report a threshold or logical error rate
5. *Do not* compare against classical algorithms solving the same problem
6. If a classical comparison exists, use the worst classical algorithm available

This is not fraud — it's the standard methodology of the field. But it creates a systematic optimism bias. Every architecture looks better when compared against other architectures that share the same assumptions, rather than against the null hypothesis: "could a classical computer do this faster or more reliably?"

### 1.2 The Straw-Man Classical Baseline Problem

When classical baselines *are* used, they frequently take the form:

- "We compared our quantum algorithm against a brute-force classical search" (ignoring decades of optimized classical heuristics)
- "Our quantum circuit achieves X; classical simulation requires exponential resources" (ignoring approximate classical methods)
- "Quantum advantage demonstrated at 53 qubits" (when classical supercomputers can simulate the same circuit with tensor network methods)

Scott Aaronson has written extensively about this. The quantum computing field has a credibility problem with classical baselines, and the few groups that benchmark honestly stand out.

## 2. The Ultrametric_v2 Approach

### 2.1 What We Did

Sprint 5 of ultrametric_v2 tested the ternary ($p = 3$) Bruhat-Tits tree encoding against:

| Benchmark | Description | Why It Matters |
|:----------|:------------|:---------------|
| **Uncorrelated (i.i.d.) noise** | Each qubit experiences independent random errors | This is the standard noise model in QC. If your architecture only works under correlated noise, you need to say so. |
| **Correlated noise** | Errors are spatially and temporally correlated | The regime where ultrametric error confinement *should* work — because tree branches create natural decorrelation boundaries. |
| **Classical tree baseline** | A classical tree structure encoding the same logical information, with the same noise, but no quantum gates | The honest null hypothesis: "Does the tree help because it's a tree (classical) or because it's quantum?" |

### 2.2 The Results (Honest)

| Noise Regime | Ternary Tree | Classical Tree Baseline | Winner |
|:-------------|:-------------|:------------------------|:-------|
| **Uncorrelated (i.i.d.)** | LER similar to baseline | Comparable | Tie (tree loses its advantage) |
| **Correlated** | LER drops by up to 48$\times$ (with $q$-ary scatter) | Error accumulates linearly | Tree |

**The honest null result:** Under uncorrelated noise, the tree encoding provides no advantage over a classical tree baseline. The geometric protection only activates when noise is correlated — i.e., in realistic physical settings where thermal fluctuations, cross-talk, and environmental drift create spatial and temporal error correlations.

### 2.3 Why This Is a Credibility Asset

Reporting the null result has three strategic benefits:

1. **Scientific integrity signaling.** If QWAV only reported positive results, skeptical readers would assume cherry-picking. Reporting the i.i.d. null result demonstrates that the validation process is honest — it distinguishes between conditions where the architecture works and conditions where it doesn't.

2. **Pre-registration alignment.** The credential document (P25) includes 5 pre-registered falsifiable predictions. Honest benchmarking is the same principle applied to computational validation: specify in advance what counts as success and failure, then report both.

3. **Differentiation from the field.** Most QC groups do not report negative results. Doing so voluntarily makes QWAV's computational claims more trustworthy than the industry baseline — even without peer review.

### 2.4 The Deeper Strategic Point

The result that the tree loses at uncorrelated noise but wins at correlated noise is *more* informative than a result that the tree wins everywhere. It tells you *where* the architectural advantage comes from (the strong triangle inequality suppresses correlated errors by geometrically confining them) and *where* it doesn't apply (random uncorrelated errors, where no geometry can help).

This is exactly the kind of result that makes a paradigm proposal credible: it doesn't claim to solve everything; it claims to solve a specific class of problems (correlated errors in quantum circuits) using a specific mechanism (ultrametric error confinement).

## 3. Who Else Benchmarks Honestly

### Groups with Credible Benchmarking Practices

| Group / Researcher | Benchmarking Practice | Relevance |
|:-------------------|:----------------------|:----------|
| **ETH Zürich (Troyer Group)** | Classical tensor network methods benchmarked against quantum advantage claims. Demonstrated that many "quantum advantage" results are within reach of optimized classical tensor contractions. | Gold standard for honest classical benchmarking. |
| **JQI / U Maryland** | Quantum advantage verification protocols that require classical baselines as part of the experimental design. | Methodology template for pre-registered benchmarking. |
| **IBM Quantum** | Publishes both quantum and classical benchmarks for their devices, including honest assessments of when classical simulation outperforms their hardware. | Industry example — shows that honest benchmarking is good PR, not just good science. |
| **Google Quantum AI** | The 2019 "quantum supremacy" claim was followed by extensive classical benchmarking (IBM's counter, then improved tensor network methods) that ultimately narrowed but didn't close the gap. The public debate improved the field's benchmarking standards. | Demonstrates that even contested claims advance the field if the methodology is public. |

### Common Failure Modes (Avoid These)

| Failure Mode | Example | QWAV's Mitigation |
|:-------------|:--------|:------------------|
| **Compare only against other quantum architectures** | "Our qubit has 99.9% fidelity vs. 99.5% for competing qubits" | Always include a classical tree baseline. |
| **Use a straw-man classical baseline** | "Classical brute force requires 10$^{20}$ operations" | Use the best available classical method for the specific problem. |
| **Report only the best-performing condition** | Cherry-pick the noise regime where the architecture shines | Report both i.i.d. and correlated results. |
| **Omit error bars or variance** | Single-point estimates without uncertainty quantification | Report full LER distributions, not just means. |
| **Claim "quantum advantage" without defining the classical baseline** | "Our architecture achieves X" without "compared to Y" | Every QWAV result explicitly states the comparator. |
