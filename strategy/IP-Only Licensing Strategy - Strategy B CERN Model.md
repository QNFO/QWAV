# STRATEGY B: IP-Only / Licensing Model (The CERN Approach)

**Purpose:** An alternative to building hardware directly. Instead of raising $300M–$700M to build quantum processors, raise $200K–$550K to file strong patents + run key validation experiments, then license the IP to companies that already have fabrication and cryogenic infrastructure. This is the "CERN model" applied at startup scale — develop the technology, protect the IP, and let others build the hardware.

**Key insight:** R&D sufficient for solid patent protection costs a fraction of building a company. The capital journey is 0.1–1% of the hardware path. Revenue arrives sooner, dilution is minimal, and the risk profile is dramatically lower.

**Reference:** This strategy is informed by CERN's Knowledge Transfer group, which spends ~$1.2B/year on research, commercializes nothing directly, and generates enormous value through open innovation licensing. The same principles apply at micro-scale.

|:----------|:----------------------------|:--------------------------------|
| **What you build** | Quantum processors, cryogenic systems, fabrication facilities | Patent portfolio, validation data, licensing agreements |
| **Phase 0 cost** | $100K–$500K | $200K–$550K |

| **Timeline to first revenue** | Year 4–7 | Year 2–4 |
| **Revenue model** | Hardware sales, cloud access, consulting | IP licensing royalties, strategic consulting, R&D grants |
| **Risk profile** | Binary — technical validation is all-or-nothing for the company | Lower — IP can be licensed for narrower applications even if full hardware path is long |
| **Ceiling** | €1B+ company | €50M–€500M (licensing revenue or acquisition by hardware company) |
| **Founder ownership** | 15–30% (heavily diluted across multiple rounds) | 60–80% (minimal dilution) |

**Neither strategy is "better" — they serve different founder goals.** Strategy A maximizes upside (€1B+) but requires massive capital and founder dilution. Strategy B minimizes risk and capital requirements but caps the upside. The choice depends on: what kind of company do you want to build, and how much risk are you willing to carry alone?

## The CERN Model, Applied at Micro-Scale

### How CERN Does It

CERN spends ~$1.2 billion per year on fundamental physics research. It does not commercialize anything directly. Instead, through its Knowledge Transfer (KT) group:

1. **Patents** are filed on technologies developed during research (detectors, magnets, computing, medical imaging)
2. **Publications** disseminate the underlying science openly
3. **Industry partnerships** bring companies in to co-develop applications
4. **Licensing agreements** generate revenue from companies that commercialize CERN technologies
5. **Revenue is reinvested** into further research

The key: CERN's value is in the **IP and knowledge**, not in building commercial products. The same model works at startup scale — you don't need a $1.2B budget to apply the principles.

## How Licensing Revenue Works (Without Building Hardware)

### Licensing Models

| Model | How It Works | Typical Terms | Best For |
|:------|:-------------|:--------------|:---------|
| **Royalty-based** | Licensee pays 3–8% of revenue from products incorporating licensed IP | 3–8% of licensee's revenue; minimum annual royalties | Broad architecture patents (UQC, QRC) |
| **Fixed-fee + royalty** | Upfront payment + ongoing royalties | $100K–$1M upfront + 2–5% royalties | Risk-sharing; provides near-term cash |
| **Per-unit** | Licensee pays per processor/chip manufactured | $10–$1,000 per unit depending on processor value | Hardware-specific implementations |
| **Cross-licensing** | Exchange IP rights with another patent holder | No cash; mutual access to each other's patents | Strategic partnerships with complementary tech |
| **Patent sale** | Sell the patent outright to a company | $500K–$10M+ depending on patent strength and market | Exit strategy; one-time liquidity |

### Who Would License Ultrametric Computing IP?

| Licensee Type | Why They'd License | Estimated Deal Size |
|:--------------|:-------------------|:--------------------|
| **Quantum hardware startup** (needing differentiation from surface-code mainstream) | UQC offers passive fault tolerance — a differentiator that attracts investors and customers | $50K–$500K/year in royalties (pre-revenue startup) |
| **Established quantum company** (Google, IBM, Quantinuum) seeking to expand IP portfolio | Defensive licensing — prevent competitors from claiming the ultrametric approach | $500K–$5M/year or one-time acquisition of patent |
| **Enterprise AI company** needing explainable AI for regulatory compliance | Q-PNA / glass-box AI patents enable auditable AI products | $100K–$1M/year in royalties |
| **Defense contractor** (Lockheed, Raytheon, BAE) needing quantum advantage | Ultrametric cryptography or sensing applications | $500K–$5M/year or research contract |
| **Semiconductor foundry** (TSMC, Intel, Samsung) exploring post-CMOS architectures | UQC architecture as a compute paradigm for beyond-silicon era | $1M–$10M/year or strategic partnership |

## The Phase 0 Plan for IP-Only

### Month 1–3: Patent Strengthening
- Engage patent attorney specializing in quantum computing
- Convert 2–4 provisional patents to full PCT applications
- File in US, EU (EPO), and PCT (international)
- Conduct freedom-to-operate analysis
- **Cost:** $30K–$50K

### Month 2–4: Secure Experimental Collaborator
- Outreach to 10–20 NV center labs (see Experimental Validation Roadmap - Ultrametric Quantum Computing.md for target list and Template D for email)
- Propose Experiment 1A: ultrametric distance property demonstration
- Negotiate collaboration terms (co-authorship, IP sharing)
- **Cost:** $3K–$5K (travel + materials)

### Month 4–9: Run Tier 1 Validation
- Execute Experiment 1A on NV center platform
- Collect data, analyze, write paper
- Submit to arXiv (open-access preprint)
- **Cost:** $25K–$30K

### Month 6–12: Build Licensing Pipeline
- Attend 3–5 quantum computing conferences (APS March Meeting, Quantum.Tech, IEEE Quantum Week)
- Present results; network with quantum hardware companies
- Direct outreach to 20+ potential licensees
- Begin licensing negotiations
- **Cost:** $30K–$50K (travel + legal)

### Month 9–18: First Licensing Revenue
- Close first licensing deal (likely a small quantum startup)
- Use revenue to fund further R&D → stronger patents → more licensing
- **Cost:** $0 (revenue-generating)

### Total Phase 0 Budget: $200K–$550K

## The EWOR Fit (Strategy B)

Strategy B makes the EWOR application stronger because:

1. **The ask is proportionate.** "Fund the R&D that makes the IP licensable" is a €200K–€500K ask — within EWOR's funding range. "Fund a $300M hardware company" is not.

2. **The timeline matches.** EWOR's program is ~12 months. In 12 months, Strategy B produces: stronger patents, experimental validation data, a submitted paper, and a licensing pipeline. Strategy A produces: a validation experiment and a plan for raising $2M–$5M.

3. **The founder fit is better.** The user is an ideas person and introvert. Licensing negotiations are still external engagement, but they're one-on-one technical discussions with companies that already understand quantum computing. It's a different kind of "selling" than pitching VCs.

4. **The risk is lower for EWOR.** If the IP doesn't license, the patents and publications still have value. The "failure state" is a strong patent portfolio that could be sold or licensed later. The "failure state" of a hardware company is a lab full of equipment and no revenue.

5. **The long-term optionality is preserved.** Strategy B doesn't preclude Strategy A later. If licensing generates significant revenue, that revenue can fund a transition to hardware development. You're not choosing forever — you're choosing the first step.

## Risks of Strategy B

| Risk | Probability | Mitigation |
|:-----|:-----------|:-----------|
| **No licensees interested** — quantum hardware companies develop their own approaches or license from others | Medium | Broaden licensing outreach to AI companies, defense contractors, semiconductor foundries; the IP has applications beyond quantum hardware |
| **Patents challenged or invalidated** — prior art or obviousness findings | Low-Medium | Strong prior art search; detailed claims; build on the mathematical novelty (p-adic geometry is genuinely novel in computing) |
| **Validation experiments fail** — ultrametric property not demonstrated in NV centers | Medium | Pivot: publish the null result; refine the theory; the patents may still have value for the mathematical framework even without experimental validation |
| **Revenue insufficient to sustain operation** — licensing fees too small to cover costs | Medium | Supplement with consulting and government grants; keep burn rate low (solo founder model) |
| **Key-person risk** — the IP is tightly coupled to the founder's expertise | Medium-High | Document everything; build the patent portfolio so the IP is legally protected independent of the founder |

## Which Strategy Should You Choose?

| If you want... | Choose... |
|:---------------|:----------|
| To build a €1B+ company and control the full stack | Strategy A (Hardware) |
| To prove the concept with minimal capital and retain ownership | Strategy B (IP-Only) |
| To start with IP licensing and transition to hardware later | Strategy B → then Strategy A |
| To maximize near-term probability of revenue | Strategy B |
| To maximize long-term valuation | Strategy A |

**Recommendation for current stage:** Start with Strategy B. Use EWOR funding ($200K–$500K) to build the patent portfolio + run Tier 1 validation + establish a licensing pipeline. If licensing revenue grows to $1M+/year, you have the choice to: (a) continue as an IP licensing business, (b) use revenue to fund hardware development (transition to Strategy A), or (c) sell the IP portfolio to a quantum hardware company.

Strategy B is the lower-risk first step that preserves all options. Strategy A is the higher-reward destination that Strategy B can fund.
